A Memory That Never Left
The white sands of Binh Thuan, and one fruit that changed everything.

My first memory of Binh Thuan was a family pilgrimage to Dinh Thay Thim — a sacred temple in the old land of scorching white sand and ocean wind. The heat was relentless. And then, someone handed me a dragon fruit, freshly cut under the open sky.

In that moment, every drop of that fruit made the heat disappear. Cool, sweet, and somehow deeply Vietnamese. I was just a child, but I never forgot it. Along the roadsides, mountains of dragon fruit were stacked beside the fields — selling for as little as 500 to 3,000 VND per kilo. Beautiful, abundant, and almost given away.

"Under that burning sun, one bite of dragon fruit made the heat disappear. I never forgot that feeling."
💡
The Moment Everything Changed
2024. Phan Thiet. A mountain of fruit — and one question.

In 2024–2025, work brought me back to Binh Thuan — to Phan Thiet, Ham Thuan Bac and Nam, Mui Ke Ga, and La Gi. And there they were again: thousands of dragon fruits stacked into mountains beside the fields. I walked over and asked the farmers.

They told me these were the rejects — bruised skin, uneven shape, too small for export standards. Traders would come and take some for local markets. The rest? Left to rot, or fed to cattle. Hundreds of thousands of tonnes every season. Agricultural waste — from fruit that was still sweet, still nutritious, still delicious.

"Why? A single word that launched a crazy idea: turn something thrown away into something of real value."
Founder tại vùng trồng thanh long Bình Thuận — nơi hàng nghìn quả loại B bị bỏ đi mỗi ngày
🔥
8 Months of Beautiful Failure
Burnt batches. Sleepless nights. And a lot of jam poured down the drain.

The R&D process was not glamorous. Batch after batch was thrown away — burnt when I fell asleep monitoring the temperature, too runny, too thick, the wrong balance of sweetness. I once calculated that the amount I wasted was enough to buy a brand-new SH 350i motorcycle.

There were moments when friends tasted early versions and gave honest, brutal feedback. We thought we had it — and then we didn't. There were nights of real doubt: had we chosen the wrong path entirely? But something stubborn kept us going. The vision was too clear to abandon.

After conversations with friends in La Gi and months of trial and error, the answer finally came: remove the refined sugar entirely and replace it with fresh sugarcane molasses. Remove the industrial additives and replace them with green apple and fresh lemon juice. Let the fruit speak for itself, at the right temperature, by hand.

"I don't know how many batches I threw away. But I refused to give up — because the fruit deserved better."
